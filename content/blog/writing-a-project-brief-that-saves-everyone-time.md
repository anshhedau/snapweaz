A great brief is the single highest-leverage document in any creative project. Here is what belongs in one.

## Why Briefs Matter More Than Decks

Most projects that go off the rails do so because the brief was vague, missing, or written after the fact. A clear brief saves weeks of rework and protects the relationship between client and studio.

## The Six Sections Every Brief Needs

Keep briefs short. A two page document is almost always better than a twenty page one.

- The problem in one sentence
- Who the audience is
- What success looks like in numbers
- What is in and out of scope
- Timeline and key milestones
- Budget range or constraints

## Write the Brief Together

The best briefs are written collaboratively in a single working session. The studio asks questions, the client answers, and the document forms in real time. By the end, both sides know what is being built.

## Decide Who Owns the Decisions

Every brief should name the single person on the client side who can approve work. Decisions by committee are the most expensive failure mode in creative work.

## Revisit the Brief Often

A brief is not a sacred text. As the project moves, the team learns things that change the plan. Update the brief, share the new version, and keep everyone aligned.
