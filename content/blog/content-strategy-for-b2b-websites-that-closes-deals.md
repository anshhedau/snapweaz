Most B2B sites are written for the company, not the buyer. Here is how to flip that around.

## Write for the Buyer, Not the Boardroom

Most B2B copy reads like an internal memo. The buyer does not care about your mission statement. They care whether you can solve their problem, on time, and on budget.

## Map Content to the Buying Stages

A real B2B buying process has at least three stages. Awareness, evaluation, and decision. Each needs different content.

- Awareness content that names the problem
- Evaluation content that compares approaches
- Decision content that removes risk
- Post-purchase content that supports the user

## Specifics Beat Adjectives

Words like 'innovative' and 'world-class' are weightless. Numbers, names, and screenshots are heavy. Replace the adjectives with proof and the page gets stronger.

## Make the Sales Team a Source

The best B2B content sits in the inbox of your sales team. Every objection they hear is a future blog post. Every question they answer twice is a future page on the site.

## Update Often, Prune Often

Stale content erodes trust. A quarterly review of the top pages keeps the site sharp. Remove anything outdated. Refresh anything that still ranks but reads dated.
