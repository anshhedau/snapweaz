Headless CMS solves real problems but introduces new ones. Here is how to decide whether your team needs one.

## What Headless Actually Means

A headless CMS separates the content from the presentation. Content lives in a database accessed by API, and the site or app rendering it is its own project. This decoupling is powerful but not free.

## When It Pays Off

Headless makes sense when content powers more than one surface, when the editorial team needs structured workflows, or when the engineering team wants total control over the frontend.

- One content pool powering many surfaces
- Strict editorial workflows
- A frontend team that wants modern tools
- Performance requirements a traditional CMS cannot meet

## When It Does Not

A small marketing site with three editors and one designer does not need a headless CMS. The simplest setup that works is usually the right one. Headless adds infrastructure, hosting, and integration work that has to be justified.

## Pick the CMS for the Editors, Not the Engineers

Engineers will integrate any API. Editors will quit if the authoring experience is bad. The CMS choice should be driven by the people who will live in it every day.

## Plan for Preview and Caching

The two technical headaches with headless are preview environments and cache invalidation. Solve them on day one and the experience stays good as the site grows.
