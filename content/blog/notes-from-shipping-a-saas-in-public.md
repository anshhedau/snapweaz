Lessons from launching a software product transparently, including what we got right, what we got wrong, and what we would do again.

## Public Building Forces Clarity

When you share progress weekly, you cannot hide behind vague roadmaps. The discipline of explaining the work to strangers sharpens the work itself.

## Audience Compounds Slowly

The first months of building in public are quiet. The compound effect kicks in around month six, when the back catalog of posts starts pulling new readers and customers in every week.

## Pick One Channel and Commit

Posting everywhere at once dilutes the message and burns out the team. Pick the single channel where your audience lives, and post consistently for a year before judging the result.

- Weekly newsletter for depth
- Twitter or LinkedIn for reach
- YouTube for product walkthroughs
- A changelog for transparency

## Customers Become Co-Builders

When users see the process, they start contributing ideas, bug reports, and even copy. The relationship shifts from buyer and seller to collaborators with shared interest in the outcome.

## Some Things Are Better Private

Building in public does not mean every decision is shared. Pricing experiments, hiring, and difficult customer situations are usually better kept off the timeline.
